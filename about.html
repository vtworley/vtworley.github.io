About

I’m a doctoral candidate in the Department of Government and Politics at the University of Maryland, College Park, with an expected defense date of Spring 2027. My work sits at the intersection of federalism, judicial politics, and state politics. I study how states, through their attorneys general, have engaged with the federal government over time: what has changed, why it changed, and what those changes have produced.

My dissertation, Vertical Litigation, Political Polarization, and State Attorneys General, examines state-led intergovernmental litigation against the federal government and the role that partisan polarization and federal devolution have played in driving that process. Other ongoing projects include a measure of state attorney general institutional power, an analysis of the federal government's own use of intergovernmental litigation, and a measure of state-level attention to the Supreme Court.

I’ve taught courses on constitutional law, judicial politics, and democratic theory and advised research capstone projects at the University of Maryland and Marymount University. Before pursuing my PhD, I completed a BA in Political Science and an MAT in Secondary Social Studies Education at Coastal Carolina University. I also hold an MA in Government and Politics from the University of Maryland.

When I’m not writing or teaching away, I love to explore Washington, DC and find the diveiest of dive bars, strongest cups of coffee, and cutest dogs in my neighborhood. I also love making time to go home to South Carolina (the real peach state, I will die on this hill) to go to the beach with my mom or fishing with my dad. I also love to sew and read, as I'm firmly convinced your late 20s are for grandma hobbies, and am willing to see any concert under $25.
